In a high-concurrency environment like event registration, "ties" are the ultimate test of that determinism. Here are three specific scenarios where the system has to break a tie:

1. The "Split-Second" Final Seat
Imagine an event has exactly one seat left. Jane and another user both hit the "Confirm Registration" button at what appears to be the exact same time.
The Conflict: On a network level, these requests might arrive at the server within microseconds of each other.
The Mo Perspective: If the system doesn't have a clear, documented tie-breaking rule (like a sequential database ID or a global atomic clock), the result can feel arbitrary or like a "silent failure" to the person who lost the seat.
2. Identical Waitlist Priority
Suppose your system allows users to have "Priority Status" (e.g., VIPs or early-access members).
The Conflict: Two users with the exact same priority level join the waitlist. A spot opens up. If the system only looks at "Priority Level," it sees two identical candidates.
The Mo Perspective: Mo needs to know why the other person got the spot. A deterministic constraint here might involve a secondary "tie-breaker" attribute, such as the exact millisecond of account creation or alphabetical order, which must be explicitly citeable in the system's explanation.
3. Simultaneous Conflict Resolution
Jane loves "automatic conflict handling." Imagine she is on a waitlist for Event A, which starts at 2:00 PM. She is already registered for Event B, which also starts at 2:00 PM.
The Conflict: At the exact moment a spot opens for Event A, the system's automated logic tries to "promote" her from the waitlist while simultaneously trying to "cancel" her conflicting registration for Event B.
The Mo Perspective: If two automated processes (the "Promoter" and the "Canceller") trigger at once, which one "wins" the write-access to the database? Without determinism, the system might crash or end up in an inconsistent state where she is registered for both or neither.

LLM complete response: When Jane hands over the keys to the system for "automatic promotion," she’s trusting it to be her personal assistant. But in software, "automatic" often means the system is making assumptions. For Mo, these assumptions are dangerous if they aren't explained; for Jane, they're dangerous if they create a mess she has to clean up later.

Here are some "weird" edge cases where the logic might trip over itself:

1. The "Circular Dependency" Trap
Imagine Jane is on the waitlist for Workshop A (which requires Intro Class B as a prerequisite). She is also on the waitlist for Intro Class B.
The Scenario: A spot opens in the advanced Workshop A. The system's "promotion logic" triggers. It sees Jane is next in line, but then it checks her credentials and sees she hasn't completed Intro Class B yet (because she's still on that waitlist).
The Failure: Does the system skip her? Does it promote her and then "silently fail" the registration later? Or does it promote her for both simultaneously, risking a state where she’s in the advanced class without the foundation?
2. The "Ghost Spot" Race Condition
This happens when the system's "Available Seats" counter gets out of sync with the actual "Registered Users" list.
The Scenario: A user cancels their registration at the exact same millisecond the system is running a database backup or a batch update.
The Failure: The system "sees" a vacancy and promotes Jane. However, because of a slight lag in database replication, the "seat" wasn't actually committed as empty yet. Jane receives a "You're In!" notification, but when she logs in, the system shows her as "Waitlisted" again because the seat "disappeared." This is a nightmare for Mo's need for deterministic behavior.
3. The "Time-Zone Teleportation" Conflict
Jane is a busy professional who travels. She registers for an event in New York (EST) at 10:00 AM. While on the waitlist for a second event in London (GMT), a spot opens up.
The Scenario: If the system’s "Automatic Conflict Handling" doesn't account for UTC offsets or travel time, it might see a 10:00 AM and a 3:00 PM event as perfectly fine.
The Failure: Jane gets promoted to an event she physically cannot attend because the system only checked the numerical time, not the absolute time. Now she’s registered for something she’ll have to manually cancel, violating her "no notification/task overload" preference.
4. The "Expired Payment Token" Loop
Jane might have a saved credit card for "automatic" payments upon promotion.
The Scenario: A spot opens up, the system promotes her, and it attempts to charge the card. The card is expired.
The Failure: Since Jane wants "automatic conflict handling" and "no overload," the system might try to "fix" it by retrying. If it fails three times, does it kick her off the list? Mo would demand to know exactly why she was kicked, while Jane would be annoyed she had to deal with a "system failure" she thought was automated.
